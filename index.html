Abstract

Research on mobile assistive systems for blind and low-vision (BLV) individuals spans five decades, beginning in 1976. However, mobile navigation systems that mimic how guide dogs assist BLV people remain rare. One critical missing component is the lack of understanding of how guide dogs and BLV handlers navigate real-world environments. In this paper, we address this gap by defining a guide-dog–inspired navigation setup based on a comprehensive human study involving interviews with 26 guide dog handlers, 2 white cane users, 5 guide dog trainers, 1 O\&M trainer, 10 hours of observing guide dog–assisted walking, and 3 hours of blindfolded walking sessions guided by six different guide dogs.

Based on these findings, we developed GuideNav, a vision-only, teach-and-repeat navigation system. Similar to how guide dogs are trained and assist their handlers, GuideNav autonomously repeats a path demonstrated by a sighted person using a robot. The system builds a topological representation of the taught route, integrates visual place recognition with temporal filtering, and employs a learned relative pose estimator to provide input to the controller—all without relying on heavy, power-hungry sensors such as LiDAR.

In field tests, GuideNav consistently achieved kilometer-scale route following across five outdoor environments, maintaining reliability despite noticeable appearance changes between teach and repeat runs. A user study with 3 guide dog handlers and one guide dog trainer confirmed the system’s feasibility, marking (to our knowledge) the first demonstration of a quadruped mobile system retrieving a path in a manner comparable to guide dogs.

Related Links

There's a lot of excellent work that was introduced around the same time as ours.

Progressive Encoding for Neural Optimization introduces an idea similar to our windowed position encoding for coarse-to-fine optimization.

D-NeRF and NR-NeRF both use deformation fields to model non-rigid scenes.

Some works model videos with a NeRF by directly modulating the density, such as Video-NeRF, NSFF, and DyNeRF

There are probably many more by the time you are reading this. Check out Frank Dellart's survey on recent NeRF papers, and Yen-Chen Lin's curated list of NeRF papers.
